## SECTION 1: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### 1.1 Business Identity

Burger Empire (operated by **A1 Foods**, a Partnership firm) is Gwalior's only multi-outlet homegrown burger QSR chain. Founded in 2018 by **Animesh Gupta and Rahul Saraogi** (originally from Jhansi, UP), it grew to 6–8 Gwalior outlets and 19+ national outlets across 15+ cities. The brand pioneered affordable, veg-forward fast food for Gwalior's student and young professional demographic, and built one of the strongest local restaurant presences on Zomato (15,800+ reviews at City Center) and Swiggy (8,000+ ratings).

**2025 was its best year ever — and the year its crisis began.**

### 1.2 What Went Wrong — The Two-Sentence Summary

On **February 2, 2024**, a Brahmin customer in Gwalior ordered a Veg Burger via Zomato from Burger Empire and received a non-veg burger. Burger Empire denied responsibility in court. That denial, combined with a formal consumer forum verdict (March 2025), national media coverage, algorithmic demotion on Zomato, and the simultaneous arrival of Burger King in BB's core territory — created a compounding crisis that cut revenue by 30% and Zomato orders by 72% within one year.

## SECTION 3: THE LEGAL CASE — FULL DEEP-DIVE: CC/137/2024

> **This section consolidates all available information from Free Press Journal (both articles), LiveLaw, Law Trend, and the complainant's advocate. It is the most important section for understanding the current crisis.**

### 3.1 The Incident — What Happened (February 2, 2024)

**Time:** February 2, 2024 (exact day)
**Complainant:** Ashish Sharma, resident of Thatipur area, Gwalior (also described as Kumharpura in one source — Kumharpura is a locality within the Thatipur zone). Identifies as **"Hindu Kattar Brahman"** — for whom consuming non-vegetarian food violates deep religious belief.
**His Order via Zomato:**
- Veg Chilli Loaded Burger (from Burger Empire)
- Chocolava cake (from Burger Empire)
- Total order value: **Rs.175**

**Delivery:** Food arrived within **20 minutes** of placing the order.

**What was found upon opening:**
- The Veg Chilli Loaded Burger contained **pieces of meat (non-veg components)** upon inspection
- The second item delivered was also incorrect — **not the Chocolava cake** ordered
- Sharma identified the issue, and along with his friends present, **filmed a video documenting the delivery foul-up**. The video went viral on social media platforms.

**Complainant's immediate reaction:**
- Raised the issue immediately with **Zomato customer care**
- Also complained directly to **Burger Empire** — neither party took meaningful action at that stage
- Despite complaint: "the company did not take any action in the matter" — per FPJ article (Feb 6, 2024)

### 3.2 Zomato's Initial Response

Zomato took the following steps after the initial complaint:

| Action | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Refund issued** | Rs.175 — full order value refunded to complainant |
| **Discount coupon added** | Rs.500 bonus/extended credit added to account |
| **Platform threat** | Zomato declared in its complaint response that **Burger Empire "would be dismissed from the platform"** |

**Key observation:** Zomato's "dismissal" statement was made informally to the complainant as part of complaint resolution — it was NOT a publicly announced action. BB remained listed on Zomato through 2025, suggesting either: (a) the dismissal was not executed, or (b) a partial/soft demotion was applied algorithmically rather than a full delisting.

### 3.3 Burger Empire's Initial Response

Per FPJ (Feb 6, 2024) report: **Burger Empire took no meaningful action in response to the complainant's initial complaint.** This inaction directly precipitated Sharma escalating to the consumer forum.

### 3.4 Legal Notice — Ignored

After Zomato's partial compensation failed to satisfy Sharma (believing monetary compensation insufficient for religious/community dignity violation), he sent a **formal legal notice directly to Burger Empire** through his advocate Aditya Sharma. **Burger Empire gave NO REPLY to this legal notice.** The absence of response to a legal notice is itself a legally significant failure — it demonstrated either lack of legal counsel, negligence, or deliberate avoidance. Sharma then filed the formal consumer complaint.

### 3.5 Consumer Forum Case Filing

**Case Number:** CC/137/2024
**Filed by:** Ashish Sharma, through Advocate Aditya Sharma
**Filed against:** (1) Zomato Private Limited (2) Burger Empire restaurant (A1 Foods)
**Forum:** District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission, Gwalior, Madhya Pradesh
**Bench:** Shri Rajendra Prasad Sharma (President) + Shrimati Suman Gaur Pandey (Member) + Shri Revati Raman Mishra (Member)

**Complainant's Legal Argument:**

He contended that:
1. He had clearly ordered a vegetarian burger — order details and **bill receipt clearly mentioned "Veg Chilli Loaded Burger"**
2. Receiving non-veg food as a "Hindu Kattar Brahman" caused **severe mental agony and violated his religious sentiments**
3. The error constituted **deficiency in service and unfair trade practice** under the Consumer Protection Act
4. Both Zomato (as delivery aggregator) and Burger Empire (as food preparer) bore joint responsibility
5. He had documentary evidence: **order bill receipt, the video filmed at the time of discovery, and Zomato transaction records**

### 3.6 Zomato's Defense in Court

Zomato argued:
- It is **merely an online intermediary/platform** connecting customers and restaurants
- It **should not be held accountable for service errors** made by the restaurant
- It had already discharged its duty by refunding Rs.175 and providing Rs.500 credit
- The responsibility for food preparation and packaging accuracy rests entirely with the restaurant partner

**Why this defense failed:** The Commission held that Zomato, as an aggregator that takes orders, processes payments, and manages the delivery, cannot disclaim responsibility for the entire service chain. The platform had a duty of care to ensure accurate delivery to consumers. Section 2(6) of Consumer Protection Act 2019 defines "deficiency" broadly — and the Commission found Zomato's role in the transaction made it jointly liable.

### 3.7 Burger Empire's Defense in Court ⚠ CRITICAL

Burger Empire's defense was **two-pronged and deeply damaging to their credibility:**

**Claim 1:** The order was delivered correctly — i.e., Burger Empire claimed they sent the right (vegetarian) food.

**Claim 2:** Burger Empire **suggested the complainant might have changed it himself** — effectively accusing the consumer of tampering with or fabricating the non-veg mix-up.

**Why this defense was catastrophic:**
- It directly contradicted the complainant's video evidence filmed at delivery
- Blaming a Brahmin customer for tampering with their own food to manufacture a complaint was seen as insulting to both the individual and the community
- The Commission found this defense unsubstantiated and it likely hardened the court's view of Burger Empire
- In a Hindu-majority, conservative city like Gwalior, publicly accusing a Brahmin of fabricating a veg/non-veg incident carries enormous social and reputational risk beyond the courtroom
- **Most damaging of all:** There was no mention of any SOP, kitchen protocol, or systematic safeguard that could support the "delivered correctly" claim — making it an unsupported denial

### 3.8 The Verdict — March 20, 2025

**Date:** March 20, 2025
**Finding:** Both Zomato AND Burger Empire found **guilty of deficiency in service and unfair trade practice.**

**Court's Reasoning (per Law Trend and LiveLaw reports):**

1. Zomato, as an online aggregator, had a **duty to ensure that the correct food was delivered**
2. The restaurant (Burger Empire) was **responsible for preparing and packaging the correct meal** as per the order
3. The error caused **significant emotional and religious distress**, impacting the complainant's fundamental rights as a consumer
4. The evidence (order receipt + transaction records + witness testimony) established the complainant's claim

**Orders issued:**
- **Zomato:** Pay Rs.5,000 compensation to complainant + Rs.1,000 legal costs
- **Burger Empire:** Pay Rs.5,000 compensation to complainant + Rs.1,000 legal costs

## SECTION 4: ZOMATO PLATFORM ACTIONS & ECONOMICS

### 4.1 Zomato's Formal Action Against Burger Empire

**Stated action:** After Ashish Sharma complained, Zomato's customer care team communicated that **"the restaurant would be dismissed from the platform."**

**What actually happened:**
- Burger Empire remained listed on Zomato through 2025 and into 2026 (confirmed — Zomato pages for City Center, DD Nagar, Morar, Mahalgaon all still accessible)
- However, the POS data tells a different story: **Zomato orders declined continuously from the Sept–Oct 2025 peak, with Feb 2026 at 72.1% below prior year**
- This pattern is consistent with **algorithmic soft-demotion** rather than full delisting — BB remained visible but ranked lower, received fewer organic impressions, and was deprioritized in search results

**The "dismissal" therefore appears to have been partially executed as:**
1. Priority de-ranking (lower in search results)
2. Reduced organic impressions
3. Exclusion from "Top Picks" or curated recommendation slots
4. Potentially reduced delivery rider prioritization (Zomato's June 2025 visibility plan gives paying restaurants rider priority)

## SECTION 7: ROOT CAUSE ANALYSIS

> All six root causes are operating simultaneously. The collapse is not due to any single factor but a convergence that overwhelmed a business with no defensive infrastructure.

### ROOT CAUSE #1 — SEVERITY: CRITICAL
**The Legal Verdict + Denial + Silence Trifecta**

**The Incident (Feb 2, 2024):** Brahmin customer ordered Veg Chilli Loaded Burger. Received non-veg food. Filmed video evidence immediately.

**Burger Empire's Three Fatal Decisions:**

| Decision | What BB Did | What BB Should Have Done |
|---|---|---|
| **Initial complaint response** | No meaningful action | Immediate apology, investigation, personal call from owner |
| **Legal notice response** | **IGNORED — did not reply** | Settle professionally, issue formal apology, offer meaningful redress |
| **Court defense** | Claimed food was correct; **suggested customer tampered with it himself** | Acknowledge error, present kitchen improvement protocol, show good faith |
| **Post-verdict response** | **Complete silence — zero public statement** | Public apology, transparency statement, remediation plan announced |

**Root Cause Chain:**
1. Kitchen veg/non-veg segregation failed during preparation
2. No formal protocols existed for dietary separation (no SOPs documented)
3. When incident occurred, decision was to deny rather than acknowledge
4. Denial strategy accelerated community distrust
5. National media coverage permanently indexed the verdict
6. Zomato algorithmic demotion followed media coverage
7. **The incident item was never removed — creating ongoing repeat-incident risk**

**Quantified Impact:**
- Zomato Feb 2026: -72.1% vs Feb 2025
- Swiggy Feb 2026: -60.7%
- Pickup Feb 2026: -42% (community awareness even affecting walk-ins)
- Permanent Google search results prominently showing legal liability

---

### ROOT CAUSE #2 — SEVERITY: CRITICAL
**Zomato Platform Demotion + Commission Escalation Trap**

The legal case triggered a platform demotion protocol at Zomato that evolved over 18 months into a structural economic squeeze:

**Phase 1 (2024):** Algorithmic soft-demotion after "dismissal" threat. Orders remain but organic rank reduced.

**Phase 2 (Early 2025):** Platform fee hike from Rs.5→Rs.6→Rs.10. Long-distance fee introduced. BB's margin per Zomato order compressed by Rs.20–40.

**Phase 3 (Mid-2025):** Visibility Assurance Plan introduced at Rs.6,500/month/outlet. BB's 6–8 outlets would require Rs.39,000–52,000/month just to maintain prior visibility. Non-subscribers lose rider priority and search placement.

**Phase 4 (Sep–Oct 2025):** Fee raised to Rs.12. Combined commission now capped at 30% but that still means Rs.73 gone from every Rs.245 order before any other cost.

**Root Cause Chain:**
1. Legal case trigger → Zomato "dismissal" threat (Feb–Apr 2024)
2. Soft demotion began → Swiggy drops -49.4% April 2025 (first measurable signal)
3. Platform fee hikes (July 2024 – Sep 2025) compress already-thin margins
4. Visibility Plan creates a "pay-to-be-seen" dynamic BB cannot afford without direct channel revenue
5. BK/KFC/McD pay visibility fees → rank above BB in same search results
6. February 2026: Full algorithmic collapse — 154 Zomato orders vs 551 a year prior

**National QSR Flanking — BK, KFC, McD in BB's Core Territory**

| McDonald's | Already at Shinde Ki Chhawani | Lashkar zone | Same zone — McAloo Tikki vs BB's Aloo Tikki Twist |

**The Kartik Aryan Factor:** BK's brand ambassador is Gwalior-born. In a city where local identity runs deep, having a beloved hometown celebrity endorse your competitor is a cultural flanking maneuver that money cannot easily counter. Every billboard, Instagram post, and TV ad featuring Kartik Aryan for BK is a trusted peer recommendation in Gwalior.

**The Rs.99 Problem:** BK's app-exclusive Rs.99 combos are positioned almost identically to BB's entry-tier pricing. But they come with certified veg safety, a loyalty app, and a national brand trust score. For a student choosing between the two in 2026, BK wins on every dimension except local cultural affinity — which BB has now partly forfeited through the veg/non-veg incident.

**Zomato's evidence submitted:**
- Confirmation of Rs.175 refund and Rs.500 credit

**Burger Empire's evidence submitted:**
- Claim: order was correctly fulfilled (no supporting documentation or kitchen records cited in available sources)
- Claim: complainant may have tampered with the order

**Commission's final reasoning:**
- Aggregator responsibility established under Consumer Protection Act 2019's broad "deficiency in service" definition
- Restaurant's packaging/preparation responsibility clear
- Burger Empire's "customer tampered" defense rejected: no evidence presented; video at time of delivery contradicts claim
- Both parties ordered to pay Rs.5,000 compensation + Rs.1,000 legal costs each
